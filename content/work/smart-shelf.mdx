## Overview

Smart Shelf started as a question: what would inventory management look like if
it were built for the corner store, not the warehouse? Most tools in this space
are bloated enterprise software. I wanted something a small business owner could
open on their phone and understand in thirty seconds.

## The problem

Small retailers track stock in their head, in notebooks, or in spreadsheets
nobody updates. The result is always the same: items run out silently, or money
sits frozen in shelves full of things that don't sell.

The real problem isn't counting — it's **knowing what to pay attention to**. So
the product centers on signals, not spreadsheets:

- Low-stock alerts before you run out, not after
- "Dead stock" flags for items that haven't moved in weeks
- A single daily summary instead of a dashboard you dig through

<Callout label="insight">
The hardest part wasn't building inventory tracking — it was deciding what
*not* to build. Every screen I cut made the product clearer.
</Callout>

## Technical decisions

A few choices shaped the build.

**Server-first with Next.js App Router.** Inventory data is read far more than
written, so server components keep the initial load fast and the client bundle
small.

**Supabase with Row Level Security.** Each store only ever sees its own data,
enforced at the database level rather than in app code — which is exactly where
that kind of rule belongs.

**Optimistic updates on the client.** When you mark an item restocked, the UI
reflects it instantly and reconciles with the server in the background. Stock
counting should feel as fast as thinking.

## What I learned

This was my first project pushing Supabase RLS beyond the basics, and it
reframed how I think about where logic should live. Moving authorization into
the database — instead of scattering checks across the app — made the whole
thing simpler and safer at once.

> Build the thing that answers a question, not the thing that stores data.

That line ended up guiding every feature decision after it.
